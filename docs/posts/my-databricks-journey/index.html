My Journey at Databricks

Finding Databricks

When I was working on the batch scheduling team at Facebook, I noticed something unusual about our sister team. This team was migrating all of Facebook’s data workloads from the in-house Hive analytics engine to the open-source Spark engine. I’ve heard countless times from our engineers that open-source tools simply cannot operate at the Facebook scale. This explains why Facebook is such a prolific contributor to open source. The company almost always needs to build tools from scratch to handle their unique engineering challenges and then shares these tools with the community. So why then was Facebook adopting Spark instead of building something from scratch like it usually does? This question made me start thinking there was something special about Spark. I first became interested in Databricks after meeting someone on the Spark team who used to work there. I learned from him that Databricks was founded by the professors and PhDs who created Spark. At the time, I was deciding between pursuing higher education in Computer Science and working in the software industry. Working at Databricks seemed like the perfect compromise where I could learn from professors and PhDs while still getting paid.

Joining Databricks

When I joined Databricks, it was immediately clear that the experience would be very different from Facebook. Most of the knowledge I had built up about Facebook’s internal ecosystem became worthless overnight. For 2 months I was completely lost when my colleagues used terms like K8s Daemonsets, NAT Gateways, Hive Metastore, and BoneCP. This was such a major problem for me, so whenever I heard a new term I’d jot it down. On the weekend I’d spend hours researching the new terms and summarizing what I had learned. Ramping up was difficult but I was learning so much, which is exactly what I wanted. And better yet, this time the knowledge I was gaining about open-source technology and public tooling would serve me for the rest of my career. I noticed that Databricks also heavily exposed me to trends in the data industry and other tools/companies in the market. By listening to coworker conversations I would learn about things like dbt, Snowflake, and Apache Arrow. Everyone I met at Databricks was extremely technical and invested in the company mission. Managers, engineers, sales people, and PMs would have long technical debates and there was never any topic too detailed to discuss. Even our CEO would ask questions about details like JVM startup time. In my opinion, Databricks is an unparalleled magnet for tech talent because everyone in our leadership was once a highly respected engineer. All the nerdy low-level details that people usually shrug off mattered to our management. In retrospect, I should have realized this earlier during my onsite interview when I was asked to write a program in LLVM. My mentors and managers constantly stunned me with their knowledge and brilliance. They inspired me to work really hard so that one day I could become like them. I loved this highly technical environment and the things I was learning here.

I started my Databricks journey on the Serverless team. This was a natural team to join given my prior experience in resource management. The Serverless team was born because customers were tired of babysitting their Spark clusters. Before this team existed, customers would have to manually adjust the cluster size or terminate the cluster depending on resource utilization. Also customers would have to manually recover workers in the cluster that became unresponsive due to spot instance kills, OOMs, etc. The Serverless team built cluster autoscaling, autorecovery, and autotermination features to free customers from these mundane tasks (see patent). I found those 3 features really interesting, so I volunteered to help migrate them to a new microservice to improve scalability. Soon afterwards there was a re-org and I moved to the Clusters team along with these features that I now owned.

Joining the Clusters team led to another huge phase of learning. This team was responsible for provisioning compute for our customers to run their Spark workloads. Working on this team made me fluent with Kubernetes and cloud tooling like S3, Terraform, and IAM. I was put on a new project which would move our VM acquisition and lifecycle management logic into a new microservice. This was needed to unlock the next level of scale because Databricks usage was skyrocketing and we were trying to keep up with customer demand. I got to design and build many interesting components like our VM garbage collector and our VM pool capacity management algorithm. Then I volunteered to do an emergency upgrade of our primary database from MySQL 5.6 → 5.7. This project was painful but I’m glad I did it because I truly internalized all the best practices involved in migrating an online database while still ensuring high availability. I’m sure this problem will show up again in my career. I also added the company’s first MySQL read replicas to help our persistence layer support the growing read-heavy traffic. This project taught me more about the MySQL binlog, debugging high replication lag, and tuning database performance by tweaking parameters like the transaction log disk flush delay.

My experiences at Facebook led me to form opinions about how Databricks should scale things. Because I had previously worked at a much larger company with mature tooling and engineering practices, I felt like I could see the future at Databricks. For example, I had strong opinions about introducing an event bus like Kafka instead of continuing to use the poll-and-diff pattern we commonly relied on. I also had thoughts on how to improve our configuration system at Databricks. All our configs were injected into our microservice containers via environment variables, so Kubernetes would restart the container upon any config change. We didn’t have zero downtime service restarts at the time because we served some APIs using state stored in memory, so engineers would usually bundle config changes with code changes. This meant it would typically take 2 weeks to flip a flag to enable some feature in production. Meanwhile, Facebook engineers could land any config change to prod within minutes. Databricks had tried experimenting with Launch Darkly for immediate config changes but stopped because too many outages were caused by flipping flags without proper diligence. One day I noticed our founding engineer publish a design doc for a tool to deploy emergency config changes without restarting the container. I reached out to him and we built a prototype during a hackathon that let engineers deploy codified emergency config changes to prod within 2 minutes. We won a prize for our prototype and it was soon productionized by a coworker. It quickly became a common tool our engineers used to mitigate large outages. I strongly believe that any configuration change should be immediate and easy to perform. The whole engineering org should be able to iterate quickly and feel productive. But of course, it’s also important to prevent outages by having proper tests and guardrails like canaried rollouts. I hope Databricks decides to extend the tool we built for non-emergency configs in the future.

I’m unfortunately not yet able to discuss the biggest and most interesting projects I worked on for confidentiality reasons.

Leaving Databricks

I recently decided to leave Databricks to build my own company. This was a tough decision because working at Databricks has been an amazing experience and the company has been doing extremely well. However, I’ve noticed some gaps in the dev tools market while at Databricks and now believe I have the technical chops to build my own solution. As you may have noticed by now, I optimize for learning and ownership above everything else. Building my own company will push me to the next level and I can’t wait to write about this new chapter in my career. Thanks for reading and stay tuned!
