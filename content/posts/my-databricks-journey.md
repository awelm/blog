### Finding Databricks

When I was working on batch scheduling team at Facebook, I noticed something unusual about one of our sister teams. This team was introducing Spark at Facebook and the company had even set a goal to fully deprecate Hive and migrate all workloads to Spark. The unusual thing was that Facebook had actually built Hive in-house and now they were deprecating it to use an external open-source technology. I've heard countless times from our engineers that open-source tools simply cannot operate at the Facebook scale. This is why Facebook is such a prolific contributor to open source. They almost always need to build tools from scratch to handle their unique engineering challenges and then they share their developments with the community. So why then was Facebook adopting Spark instead of building something from scratch like they usually do? There must have been something special about Spark. I'd actually tried to join the Spark team but they didn't have headcount at the time so instead I joined their sister team which built Spark's resource manager (think YARN or Mesos). I became interested in Databricks after meeting someone on the Spark team who used to work there. From him I learned that Databricks was founded by the professors and PhDs who created Spark. At the time, I was torn between pursuing higher education in Computer Science and working in the software industry. Working at Databricks seemed like the perfect compromise where I could work with professors and PhDs while still earning a salary in the industry.

### Joining Databricks

When I joined Databricks, it was immediately clear that the experience would be very different from Facebook. All the knowledge I had built up about Facebook's internal ecosystem became worthless overnight. For almost 2 months I was completely lost when my colleagues used terms like K8s Daemonsets, NAT Gateways, Hive Metastore, and BoneCP. This was such a big problem for me that whenever I heard a word I didn't know, I'd jot it down and then define the terms over the weekend. Ramping up was difficult but I was learning so much, which is exactly what I wanted. And better yet, this time the knowledge I was gaining about open-source technology and public tooling would serve me for the rest of my career. I noticed that Databricks also heavily exposed me to trends in the data industry and other tools/companies in the market. By listening to coworker conversations I would learn about things like dbt, Snowflake, and Apache Arrow. Everyone I me at Databricks was extremely smart and invested in the company's mission. Our engineers would have long technical debates and even our CEO would be concerned about details like JVM startup time. My mentors and managers constantly stunned me with their knowledge and brilliance. They inspired me to work really hard so that one day I could become like them. I loved this highly technical environment and the things I was learning there.

I started my Databricks journey on the Serverless team. This was a natural team to join given my prior experience in resource management. The Serverless team was born because customers were tired of babysitting their Spark clusters. Prior to this team, customers would have to manually adjust the cluster size or terminate the cluster depending on resource utilization. Also customers would have to manually recover workers in the cluster that became unresponsive for whatever reason (spot kill, OOM, etc). The Serverless team built cluster autoscaling, autorecovery, and autotermination features to free customers from these mundane tasks. I found those 3 features really interesting, so I volunteered to help migrate them to a new microservice to improve scalability. Soon afterwards there was a re-org and I moved to the Clusters team along with these features that I now owned.

Joining the Clusters team led to another huge phase of learning. This team was responsible for provisioning compute for our customers to run their Spark workloads. Working on this team made me fluent with Kubernetes and cloud tooling like S3, Terraform, IAM, etc. I was put on a new project which would move our VM acquisition and lifecycle management logic into a new microservice. This was needed to unlock the next level of scale because Databricks usage was skyrocketing and we were trying to keep up with customer demand. During this project I got to design and build a lot of interesting components like our VM garbage collector and our VM pool capacity management algorithm. Then I volunteered to do an emergency upgrade of our primary database from MySQL 5.6 → 5.7. This project was painful but I'm glad I did it because I truly internalized all the best practices involved in migrating an online database while still ensuring high availability. I'm sure this problem will show up again in my career. I also added the company's first MySQL read replicas to help our persistence layer support the growing read-heavy traffic. This project taught me more about the MySQL binlog, debugging high replication lag, and tuning database performance by tweaking things like disk flush rate.

My experiences at Facebook caused me to form opinions about how Databricks should scale things. Because I had worked at a much larger company with mature tooling and engineering practices, I felt like I could see the future at Databricks. For example, I would have strong opinions about introducing an event bus like Kafka instead of continuing to use the poll-and-diff pattern we commonly relied on. I also had thoughts on how to improve the configuration management at Databricks. All our configs were injected into our microservice containers via environment variables, so Kubernetes would restart the container upon any config change. We didn't have zero downtime service restarts at the time, so engineers would usually bundle config changes with code changes. This meant it would typically take 2 weeks to flip a flag to enable some feature in production. Facebook engineers could land any config change to prod within minutes. Databricks had tried experimenting with Launch Darkly for dynamic config changes but stopped because too many outages were caused by flipping flags without proper diligence. One day there was a hackathon approaching and I saw one of our founding engineers was working on a tool for deploying emergency config changes without restarting the container. I reached out to him and we built a prototype that let engineers deploy emergency config changes to prod within 2 minutes. We ended up winning a prize for our prototype and someone productionized it. Soon it became a common tool our engineers used to mitigate large outages. I strongly believe that any configuration changes should be easy to perform and immediate. The whole engineering org should be able to iterate quickly and feel productive. But of course it's also important to prevent outages by having proper tests and guardrails like canaried rollouts. In the future I hope we decide to extend the tool for non-emergency configs as well.

Unfortunately I'm not yet able to discuss the biggest and most interesting projects I worked on for confidentiality reasons.


### Leaving Databricks

Although working at Databricks was an amazing experience, I recently decided to leave in order to build my own company. I've noticed some gaps in the dev tools market while working at Databricks and I now feel that I have the technical chops to build a solution. As you may have noticed by now, I optimize for ownership and learning above everything else. Building my own company will push me to the next level and I can't wait to write about these new experiences. Thanks for reading and stay tuned!
